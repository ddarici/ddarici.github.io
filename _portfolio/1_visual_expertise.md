## Overview
This research strand sits at the intersection of medical education, cognitive neuroscience, and educational psychology. At its core, we ask: *how do anatomical experts see what novices miss?*

key publications: [<a href= "https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/0142159X.2023.2189538" target="_blank">Darici et al., 2023a</a>; <a href ="https://doi.org/10.1002/ase.2363" target="_blank">Darici et al., 2023b</a>; <a href="https://doi.org/10.1002/ase.2498" target="_blank">Bellstedt et al., 2024</a>]

## Eye-tracking to measure visual attention

We primarily focus on eye-tracking technology to record and analyse the eye movements of experts and novices as they view anatomical images, such as ultrasound scans, histological sections, radiographs, and cross-sectional anatomical structures.

This data serves four research objectives:

**1. Analyze visual search patterns**  
What eye movement signatures are associated with accurate image interpretation? Do generalizable strategies exist that transcend specific stimuli? And what do gaze patterns reveal about underlying cognitive processes?

**2. Characterize expertise differences**  
How does expert gaze differ from that of novices? How do eye movements shift as a function of task difficulty?

**3. Track developmental trajectories**  
How do visual search strategies evolve across the course of medical training? Is this development linear, or do qualitatively distinct intermediate stages emerge?

**4. Visual training through eye movements modeling examples**  
How can expert eye movements be used to guide novice learning? Which individual learner characteristics and contextual factors moderate the effectiveness?

## Educational impact
The findings from this research strand may inform the design of evidence-based teaching materials for anatomy education. The goal is to produce scalable, evidence-based visual training tools that can be integrated into existing medical courses without placing additional demands on teaching staff.
